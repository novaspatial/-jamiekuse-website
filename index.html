The Moment

Each passing elder is an irreversible loss. The tools to preserve what they carry have never been stronger.

Across Canada, we are losing language keepers at an alarming rate — and each one carries languages, ceremonies, and songs that exist nowhere else on Earth. Language is the key to everything: preserving the language preserves ceremony, ceremony carries the culture, and the culture leads back to the land. Yet for over a century, cultural documentation has been recorded in stereo — two channels that collapse a three-dimensional human experience into a flat, directionless plane. The elder's voice is present. But the space — the circle of singers, the resonance of the room, the living acoustic environment of the ceremony — is gone.

Spatial audio changes the entire premise of what an archive can be. Recorded in Dolby Atmos, each drum occupies its physical location. The resonance of the territory surrounds you. Future generations who listen to a spatial recording of their great-grandmother's voice do not hear her through a speaker — they sit beside her.

The recording becomes a place, not just a document.

Proof of Concept

It started with one album.

2026 Juno Award Nominee

Nakota Tayhunyabi

YB Nakota · 2025

Produced by the IPP team — a drum circle and healing song album, recorded and mixed in full Dolby Atmos, narrated by the artist's grandmother speaking in an endangered language. Proudly the first Indigenous language preservation and ceremony album ever captured in Dolby Atmos, it earned a Juno Award nomination and national attention from the Juno committee and CBC alike.

Immersive audio isn't just a technical achievement in this context — it's a cultural one. With that recognition came a realization: this quality and format belong to the mission of preserving culture and language — not as a one-time project, but as a national initiative.

The Program

Five pillars. One living archive.

These pillars are a menu, not a package. Every community chooses what matters most — document a language before it's lost, record the musical talent in your nation, design an immersive installation for your cultural centre, or build toward all five. Your priorities shape the program. We bring the tools, and the choice is always yours.

01

Archive

Mobile field teams deploy to reserves and communities across Canada to capture elders, ceremonies, oral histories, songs, and soundscapes in immersive Dolby Atmos audio.

02

Language

Your fluent speakers, recorded in spatial audio — giving your nation the resources to teach the language your way, in your classrooms, your programs, and your homes.

03

Music

Your traditional songs — drum circles, round dances, the voices of your singers — captured at the highest quality that exists, so they can be taught, shared, and passed to the next generation instead of lost with their keepers.

04

Education

Your recordings become teaching tools in your hands — powering your schools, your youth programs, and your language classes, with our support whenever you want it.

05

Experience

Leverage your immersive recordings to create interactive installations at cultural centres, events, and community spaces — allowing people to step inside a ceremony, a landscape, a living language.

How It Works

From first conversation to living archive.

Communities never have to figure out what to do — we bring a clear, respectful process that adapts to your needs, your protocols, and your priorities.

Step 01

The Conversation

We meet with leadership and knowledge keepers to hear what preservation means to your community — and which pillars matter most.

Step 02

The Plan

Together we design the visit: who is recorded, what is shared, and where — on territory, in your cultural centre, in the places the knowledge belongs. Every step is shaped with your knowledge keepers, leadership, and elders, guided by your community's protocols.

Step 03

Capturing History

Our remote Dolby Atmos studio comes to you. Elders, singers, drum circles, ceremonies, and the sound of the land itself — captured at the highest quality that exists.

Step 04

The Handoff

Every project ends in a formal archival handoff: your recordings, in every format, owned outright by your community — with guidance for caring for the archive across generations.

Common Questions

Your recordings, your way.

What can we do with our recordings?

Anything you choose — they're yours. Communities use their recordings to build language and culture programs for their schools and youth, archive the voices of their elders for future generations, and create interactive installations at community centres, events, and outreach programs. And you don't need it all figured out — the IPP team helps you find the most impactful ways to use your media, aligned with your goals, whether that's purely archival or building interactive educational resources.

Who owns the recordings?

Your community — solely and exclusively, by written agreement. We keep nothing, use nothing, and hold no rights to the material. Every recording is handed over in full at the end of every project.

Is any of this used for AI?

No. Your recordings are never fed into an algorithm, never used to train AI, never sold, and never collected or retained by us in any form. That commitment is made in writing before a single word is recorded.

We already have old recordings — tapes, videos, stereo files. Can you work with those?

Yes. Existing media can be restored and transformed into immersive formats — an old recording of a song, a story, or a voice can become the heart of an installation your community can step inside.

Who decides what gets recorded?

Your community does. Everything begins with consultation and consent — your leadership, knowledge keepers, and the people being recorded dictate what is captured, what is shared, and what stays private. Privacy and cultural respect come before any microphone is turned on.
